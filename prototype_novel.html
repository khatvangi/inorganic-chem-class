Student Question: "Why is CuSO₄ blue?"

Instead of "see Chapter 21", the system traces prerequisites and offers multiple explanation depths.

Feature 1: SCALE ZOOM — Choose Your Depth

DESCRIPTIVE: What happens

Copper(II) compounds are typically blue or green. When you dissolve copper sulfate in water, you get a blue solution. When you heat it, it turns white (loses water). This is a characteristic property used to identify copper ions.

STRUCTURAL: How it's arranged

In solution, Cu²⁺ forms [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ — an octahedral complex with 6 water molecules as ligands. The copper sits at the center with water molecules at the vertices of an octahedron. This geometry is key to understanding the color.

ELECTRONIC: Why it happens

Cu²⁺ is d⁹ — it has 9 d-electrons. In octahedral [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺, the d-orbitals split into t₂g (lower) and eg (higher) sets. Light absorption promotes an electron from t₂g to eg. This d-d transition absorbs orange light (~600-700 nm), so the complementary color (blue) is transmitted.

QUANTUM: Deep why

The d-d transition is formally Laporte-forbidden (no change in parity: d→d), but it occurs weakly due to vibronic coupling — molecular vibrations momentarily break the centrosymmetric geometry, mixing some p-character into the d-orbitals. The spin-allowed nature (no spin change) makes it stronger than spin-forbidden transitions. This is why Cu²⁺ complexes are pale blue, not intensely colored.

Feature 2: BRIDGE EXPLANATIONS — Connect to What You Know

Now: Why some orbitals go up in energy
The eg orbitals (dx²-y² and dz²) point directly at the ligands. When ligands approach, their electrons repel these orbitals more strongly, raising their energy. The t₂g orbitals (dxy, dxz, dyz) point between ligands, so they're repelled less.
You learned: Electronegativity affects bond polarity
↓ connects to ↓
Now: Why different ligands cause different splitting (Δ)
More electronegative ligands like F⁻ are weak-field (small Δ) because they hold their electrons tightly. π-acceptor ligands like CN⁻ are strong-field (large Δ) because they can accept electron density from metal d-orbitals, stabilizing t₂g.
You learned: Complementary colors on the color wheel
↓ connects to ↓
Now: Predicting complex color from absorption
If a complex absorbs orange (~600nm), it transmits the complementary color: blue. If it absorbs yellow-green (~550nm), it appears violet. The absorption wavelength depends on Δ, which depends on ligand field strength.
