1. Introduction: The Power of Periodicity

The periodic table is not merely a catalog of elements‚Äîit is a predictive tool of extraordinary power. When Mendeleev arranged elements by atomic mass in 1869, he left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties with remarkable accuracy. The underlying reason for this periodicity became clear only with quantum mechanics: the periodic recurrence of similar electron configurations.

In this session, we explore how atomic properties‚Äîradius, ionization energy, electron affinity, and electronegativity‚Äîvary systematically. These trends are not arbitrary; they arise from the interplay between nuclear charge, electron shielding, and orbital occupancy.

QUANTUM Foundation

All periodic trends ultimately trace back to the quantum mechanical behavior of electrons. The Schr√∂dinger equation determines orbital shapes and energies, which in turn govern how atoms interact.

2. Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff)

The key to understanding periodic trends is effective nuclear charge‚Äîthe net positive charge experienced by an electron after accounting for shielding by other electrons. JD Lee

Zeff = Z ‚àí S where Z = atomic number, S = shielding constant

Slater's Rules

Slater developed empirical rules to estimate shielding constants:

Slater's Rules (click to expand)
  1. Write electron configuration in groups: (1s)(2s,2p)(3s,3p)(3d)(4s,4p)...
  2. Electrons to the right contribute 0 to shielding
  3. Other electrons in the same group: 0.35 (except 1s: 0.30)
  4. Electrons in (n-1) shell: 0.85 for s/p, 1.00 for d/f
  5. Electrons in (n-2) and lower: 1.00

Worked Example: Calculate Zeff for a 2p electron in oxygen (Z=8).

Solution

S = 2(0.85) + 5(0.35) = 1.70 + 1.75 = 3.45

Zeff = 8 ‚àí 3.45 = 4.55

4. Ionization Energy

The first ionization energy (IE‚ÇÅ) is the energy required to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous atom: Housecroft

X(g) ‚Üí X‚Å∫(g) + e‚Åª     ŒîH = IE‚ÇÅ

Higher Zeff means electrons are held more tightly, requiring more energy to remove. Thus:

  • Across a period: IE generally increases (higher Zeff)
  • Down a group: IE decreases (valence electrons farther from nucleus)

Anomalies Explained

The trend is not perfectly smooth. Two notable anomalies occur in Period 2:

Why is IE‚ÇÅ(O) < IE‚ÇÅ(N)?

Nitrogen has a half-filled 2p subshell (2p¬≥) which is relatively stable due to exchange energy. Oxygen has one electron paired in a 2p orbital (2p‚Å¥), experiencing electron-electron repulsion. This makes oxygen's outermost electron easier to remove despite higher Z.

Why is IE‚ÇÅ(B) < IE‚ÇÅ(Be)?

Beryllium's outermost electron is in a 2s orbital; boron's is in a 2p orbital. The 2p orbital is higher in energy and experiences less penetration than 2s, making it easier to remove.
