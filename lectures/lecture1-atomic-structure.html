1.1 Introduction: Hydrogenic Atoms

A hydrogenic atom is any atom or ion with a single electron, such as H, He⁺, Li²⁺, or Be³⁺. These species are important because the Schrödinger equation can be solved exactly for them, providing the foundation for understanding all atomic structure.

KEY POINT: The solutions to the Schrödinger equation for hydrogenic atoms give us atomic orbitals—mathematical functions that describe where electrons are likely to be found.

The Schrödinger equation for a hydrogenic atom is:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2\psi + V(r)\psi = E\psi$$

where the potential energy \(V(r) = -\frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}\) describes the Coulomb attraction between the electron and the nucleus of charge \(+Ze\).

The solutions factorize in spherical polar coordinates:

$$\psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\phi) = R_{nl}(r) \cdot Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$$

where \(R_{nl}(r)\) is the radial wavefunction and \(Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)\) is a spherical harmonic that describes the angular dependence.

1.2 The Born Interpretation

The wavefunction \(\psi\) itself has no direct physical meaning—it can be positive, negative, or complex. The Born interpretation tells us that the probability density is given by the square of the wavefunction:

Probability Density: $$\rho(\mathbf{r}) = |\psi(\mathbf{r})|^2$$

The probability of finding the electron in a small volume element \(d\tau\) at position \(\mathbf{r}\) is \(\rho \, d\tau\).

Figure 1.3: The Born interpretation of the wavefunction. The wavefunction ψ can be positive or negative, but ρ = |ψ|² is always non-negative. At a node (where ψ = 0), there is zero probability of finding the electron.

An important consequence is that wavefunctions can interfere. When two wavefunctions overlap in the same region of space:

  • Constructive interference: same sign → enhanced probability density
  • Destructive interference: opposite signs → reduced probability density
Figure 1.4: Constructive and destructive interference of wavefunctions. Adjust the phase difference Δφ to see how interference affects the total probability density |ψ₁ + ψ₂|².

1.3 Quantum Numbers

Three quantum numbers emerge from solving the Schrödinger equation:

Principal quantum number n:

n = 1, 2, 3, ... Determines the energy and size of the orbital. Larger n means higher energy and more diffuse orbitals.

Angular momentum quantum number ℓ:

ℓ = 0, 1, 2, ..., (n−1). Determines the shape of the orbital. We use letters: s (ℓ=0), p (ℓ=1), d (ℓ=2), f (ℓ=3).

Magnetic quantum number mℓ:

mℓ = −ℓ, ..., 0, ..., +ℓ. Determines the orientation of the orbital in space. There are (2ℓ+1) orbitals in each subshell.

Figure 1.6: Shell and subshell classification. The number of orbitals in shell n is n². Adjust n to see how subshells are organized.
NODE COUNTING: The total number of nodes (where ψ = 0) is n − 1. These divide into:
  • Radial nodes: n − ℓ − 1 (spherical surfaces where R = 0)
  • Angular nodes: ℓ (planes or cones through the nucleus)
Figure 1.7: Spherical polar coordinates (r, θ, φ). The orbital wavefunction separates into radial R(r) and angular Y(θ,φ) parts.

1.4 Energy Levels

The energy of a hydrogenic atom depends only on the principal quantum number n and the nuclear charge Z:

$$E_n = -\frac{hcR_\infty Z^2}{n^2} = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV} \cdot Z^2}{n^2}$$

where R∞ is the Rydberg constant. Key observations:

  • All energies are negative (bound states)
  • Energy is proportional to Z² (He⁺ is 4× more tightly bound than H)
  • Energy spacing decreases as n increases (levels converge to E = 0)
  • In hydrogenic atoms, all orbitals with the same n are degenerate (same energy)
Figure 1.5: Quantized energy levels for H (Z=1) and He⁺ (Z=2). The energy scales as −Z²/n², so He⁺ levels are four times deeper than H levels.

1.5 Radial Wavefunctions

The radial wavefunction Rnℓ(r) describes how the orbital's amplitude varies with distance from the nucleus. For s orbitals (ℓ = 0), R is non-zero at the nucleus. For all other orbitals (ℓ > 0), R = 0 at r = 0.

Figure 1.8: Radial wavefunctions Rn0 for 1s, 2s, and 3s orbitals. Note how the number of radial nodes (n − 1) increases with n. The horizontal axis is the scaled radius Zr/a₀.
Figure 1.9: Radial wavefunctions Rn1 for 2p and 3p orbitals. These vanish at the nucleus (r = 0) because ℓ ≠ 0.

Radial Distribution Function

The radial distribution function P(r) gives the probability of finding the electron at distance r from the nucleus (regardless of direction):

$$P(r) = r^2 |R_{nl}(r)|^2$$

For the 1s orbital, P(r) has a maximum at r = a₀/Z, where a₀ = 52.9 pm is the Bohr radius.

Figure 1.11: Radial distribution function P(r) for the 1s orbital. The most probable radius is rmax = a₀/Z.
Figure 1.12: Radial distributions for 2s and 2p. The 2s orbital has an inner maximum close to the nucleus due to its additional radial node structure.

1.6 Orbital Shapes

A boundary surface is a surface that encloses a region where there is a high probability (typically 90%) of finding the electron. The shape of this surface is determined by the angular part of the wavefunction.

s Orbitals

s orbitals (ℓ = 0) are spherically symmetric—they have the same amplitude at a given distance from the nucleus regardless of direction. They have no angular nodes.

Figure 1.13: Boundary surface of an s orbital showing 90% probability. s orbitals are spherical with no angular nodes.

p Orbitals

p orbitals (ℓ = 1) have one angular node—a nodal plane passing through the nucleus. The three p orbitals (px, py, pz) are oriented along the Cartesian axes.

Figure 1.14: Boundary surfaces of p orbitals. Each has one nodal plane through the nucleus. The different colors represent opposite phases (signs) of the wavefunction.

d Orbitals

d orbitals (ℓ = 2) have two angular nodes. There are five d orbitals with different orientations: dxy, dyz, dxz, dx²−y², and dz².

Figure 1.15: Boundary surfaces of d orbitals. Each has two nodal planes (or conic surfaces for dz²). These orbitals become important in transition metal chemistry.

f Orbitals

f orbitals (ℓ = 3) have three angular nodes and complex shapes. There are seven f orbitals, which become important in lanthanide and actinide chemistry.

Figure 1.16: Boundary surfaces of f orbitals. These have three nodal surfaces and play a key role in the chemistry of the f-block elements.

1.7 Penetration and Shielding

In many-electron atoms, electrons experience both attraction to the nucleus and repulsion from other electrons. The effective nuclear charge Zeff accounts for the screening effect of inner electrons:

$$Z_{\text{eff}} = Z - \sigma$$

where σ is the shielding constant. The closer Zeff is to Z, the more the electron experiences the full nuclear charge.

Penetration: The ability of an electron to approach the nucleus and experience the full nuclear charge. s electrons penetrate more than p, which penetrate more than d, which penetrate more than f.

This explains why, in many-electron atoms, orbitals with the same n but different ℓ are no longer degenerate. The penetration effect leads to the ordering: ns < np < nd < nf in energy for a given n.

Figure 1.10: The 1s, 2s, and 3s orbitals showing radial nodes. The 2s and 3s orbitals have inner maxima that allow them to penetrate closer to the nucleus than 2p or 3p.

Summary

  • Hydrogenic atoms have exact solutions to the Schrödinger equation
  • Three quantum numbers (n, ℓ, mℓ) define each orbital
  • Energy depends on −Z²/n² for hydrogenic atoms
  • Orbital shapes are determined by spherical harmonics
  • The radial distribution function gives the probability of finding the electron at distance r
  • Boundary surfaces typically enclose 90% probability
  • Penetration and shielding explain energy ordering in many-electron atoms
