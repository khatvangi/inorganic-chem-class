1.1 The Born Interpretation of the Wavefunction

The wavefunction \(\psi\) of an electron in an atom is a mathematical function that contains all dynamical information about the electron. However, the wavefunction itself does not have direct physical meaning—it can be positive, negative, or complex.

Probability Density: $$\rho(\mathbf{r}) = |\psi(\mathbf{r})|^2$$

The probability of finding the electron in a small volume element \(d\tau\) at position \(\mathbf{r}\) is \(\rho \, d\tau\).

An important consequence is that wavefunctions can interfere. When two wavefunctions overlap in the same region of space:

  • Constructive interference: same sign → enhanced probability density
  • Destructive interference: opposite signs → reduced probability density

1.2 Quantum Numbers and Energy Levels

Each orbital is characterized by three quantum numbers:

  • Principal quantum number (n): n = 1, 2, 3, ... — determines the shell and overall size
  • Angular momentum quantum number (l): l = 0, 1, ..., n−1 — determines the subshell shape (s, p, d, f)
  • Magnetic quantum number (mₗ): mₗ = −l, ..., +l — determines orientation
Spectroscopic notation: l = 0 → s, l = 1 → p, l = 2 → d, l = 3 → f

Energy Levels

For a hydrogenic atom (one electron, nuclear charge Z), the energy depends only on n:

$$E_n = -\frac{Z^2 R_H}{n^2} = -\frac{13.6 \, Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV}$$

Spherical Polar Coordinates

Atomic orbitals are naturally described in spherical polar coordinates (r, θ, φ), where:

  • r = radial distance from nucleus
  • θ = polar angle (from z-axis)
  • φ = azimuthal angle (in xy-plane)

1.3 Radial Wavefunctions

The wavefunction can be separated into radial and angular parts:

$$\psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\phi) = R_{nl}(r) \cdot Y_l^{m}(\theta,\phi)$$

The radial wavefunction R(r) describes how the wavefunction varies with distance from the nucleus.

Node counting: An orbital with quantum numbers n, l has:
  • Radial nodes: n − l − 1
  • Angular nodes: l
  • Total nodes: n − 1

1.4 Radial Distribution Functions

While |R(r)|² gives the probability density at distance r, we often want to know the probability of finding the electron at a distance r (in a thin shell). This is given by the radial distribution function:

$$P(r) = r^2 |R(r)|^2$$

The factor r² accounts for the increasing volume of spherical shells as r increases.

1.6 Penetration and Shielding

In many-electron atoms, the simple picture changes because electrons experience:

  • Attraction to the nucleus
  • Repulsion from other electrons (shielding)
Penetration: An electron that has significant probability close to the nucleus is said to "penetrate" the inner electron density. Better penetration means less shielding and lower energy.

Orbitals with l = 0 (s orbitals) penetrate more effectively than p orbitals, which penetrate more than d orbitals. This explains why:

  • 4s fills before 3d in transition metals
  • The effective nuclear charge Zeff differs for different subshells
Effective Nuclear Charge: $$Z_{\text{eff}} = Z - \sigma$$

where σ is the shielding constant. Better penetrating orbitals experience larger Zeff and are more tightly bound.
